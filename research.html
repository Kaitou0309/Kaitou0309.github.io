CISESS / ESSIC Research Experience

Scientific research translated into ML engineering practice

My research centers on single-source super-resolution of microwave sounder imagery. The broader engineering challenge is building reproducible data, training, and evaluation workflows for complex, high-volume scientific datasets.

Engineering contributions

The project required more than model architecture changes. I worked across the full experiment lifecycle.

Scientific Data Pipelines

Organized physically simulated low- and high-resolution AMSR-2 brightness temperature fields in HDF5, validated array structure, and prepared consistent model inputs.

Deep Learning Experiments

Developed and compared residual dense generators, composite SSIM objectives, GAN fine-tuning, and architecture ablations.

HPC Workflows

Ran GPU training and evaluation through SLURM, controlled thread and memory usage, inspected logs, and made long experiments restartable.

Quantitative Evaluation

Compared outputs with RMSE, MAE, bias, PSNR, SSIM, latency, residual maps, and spatial-frequency diagnostics.

Reproducibility

Converted exploratory notebook logic into scripts, cached predictions, documented model specifications, and standardized plotting workflows.

Technical Communication

Created architecture diagrams, convergence plots, metric comparisons, paper figures, and explanations for technical and nontechnical audiences.

Research focus

Microwave super-resolution

Recover fine-scale structure in 89 GHz microwave imagery while preserving radiometric fidelity. The work targets the coarse, scan-angle-dependent spatial resolution of cross-track microwave sounders, whose footprints broaden toward the scan edge.

Model development

Residual dense and adversarial models

Compare RDN/RRDN model depth, MAE and composite SSIM objectives, and PatchGAN-style adversarial fine-tuning with and without discriminator BatchNorm.

Validation

General and hurricane-specific evaluation

Test behavior across aggregate datasets and tropical-cyclone scenes using per-scene metrics, residual maps, and power spectral density. Qualitative applications to ATMS observations examine whether learned detail transfers to operational sensors.

Research output

Manuscript in preparation

This work is being prepared for submission to Journal of Geophysical Research: Machine Learning and Computation. I am listed as a co-author on the manuscript.
